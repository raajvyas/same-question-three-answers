  1. The Big Picture: What Is Being Regulated?

AI regulation today is trying to answer four core questions:

  1. What counts as “AI”?
  2. Where should obligations attach — the model, the deployer, or the use case?
  3. How do we manage unknown or emergent risks?
  4. How do we regulate without freezing innovation or entrenching incumbents?

Different jurisdictions answer these differently — and that’s where most of the interesting debate lies.

  1. The Three Major Governance Models

A. The EU Model — Risk-Based Statutory Regulation

The EU AI Act is the first comprehensive AI law. Its structure:

  • Prohibited uses (e.g., certain manipulative or surveillance practices)
  • High-risk systems (e.g., AI in employment, credit, medical devices)
  • General-purpose AI (GPAI) obligations (including powerful foundation models)
  • Transparency rules for some lower-risk systems

Key features: obligations include documentation, risk management, conformity assessments, incident reporting. Heavy fines for noncompliance. A centralized EU AI Office coordinates implementation.

Philosophy: Precautionary, rights-protective, and product-safety oriented.

Key debate hook: Should powerful foundation models be regulated as infrastructure, even before specific harms occur?

B. The U.S. Model — Executive + Standards-Based + Sectoral

The U.S. does not yet have a comprehensive AI statute. Instead it uses:

  • The White House Executive Order (Oct 2023)
  • Agency-level regulation (FTC, FDA, CFPB, etc.)
  • Procurement requirements
  • Technical standards via NIST

Key features: focus on testing, red-teaming, and benchmarking. Agencies must establish AI governance structures. Emphasis on civil rights, national security, and innovation.

Philosophy: Flexible, adaptive, and standards-driven rather than statute-heavy.

Key debate hook: Can voluntary standards and executive coordination substitute for legislation?

C. The Standards Layer — NIST AI Risk Management Framework

NIST’s AI RMF is not law — it’s operational guidance. It identifies AI-specific risks:

  • Dataset bias and representational harm
  • Model drift over time
  • Opacity and explainability limits
  • Emergent or unpredictable behavior
  • Environmental and systemic impacts

It organizes governance into lifecycle functions: Map risks, Measure risks, Manage risks.

Why this matters: it bridges high-level policy and engineering practice. If someone talks abstractly about “AI safety,” you can pivot to lifecycle risk management and measurable governance processes.

  1. The Core Fault Lines in AI Regulation
  1. Regulating Models vs. Regulating Uses

Should obligations apply to the model developer, the deployer, or the specific use case?

EU attaches obligations partly at the model level (GPAI). US tends to regulate use via existing sectoral law.

Insight: Model-level regulation addresses systemic risk but may be overbroad. Use-level regulation misses upstream capability risks.

  1. Innovation vs. Entrenchment

Strict compliance regimes favor large incumbents and raise entry barriers for startups. But lack of regulation encourages race-to-the-bottom deployment and externalizes harms.

Frame this as a competition-policy question, not just a safety question.

  1. Dynamic vs. Static Risk

AI systems evolve through updates, exhibit emergent behavior, and interact with social systems unpredictably. Traditional product regulation assumes fixed products and predictable failure modes.

That’s why both EU and US emphasize continuous monitoring, post-deployment reporting, and evaluation.

  1. Enforcement Reality

The hardest question isn’t writing rules — it’s enforcing them. Challenges: technical opacity, cross-border deployment, open-source models, rapid iteration cycles.

Trigger question: “What institutional capacity do regulators need to meaningfully audit foundation models?”

  1. Where the Landscape Is Moving (2024–2026 Trends)
  1. More laws globally (AI Index shows legislative surge post-ChatGPT).
  2. Increased scrutiny of frontier models (compute thresholds, systemic risk).
  3. Mandatory transparency reporting (training data summaries, safety evaluations).
  4. Procurement as regulation (governments shaping standards by buying only compliant systems).
  5. International coordination attempts (G7, OECD, UK AI Safety Summit).

The real frontier: how to govern general-purpose foundation models that can be used for both benign and harmful purposes.
